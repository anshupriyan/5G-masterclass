# 02. 5G Random Access (RACH) Procedure

Once a User Equipment (UE) has synchronized in time and frequency and read the minimum system information (MIB and SIB1) during Initial Access, it must establish a physical connection link with the network. This is accomplished via the **Random Access Channel (RACH) Procedure**. 

The RACH procedure is the first instance where a device transmits signals in the uplink to get the base station's attention.

![[RACH_Slide_1.png]]

---

## 🚦 1. Triggers of the Random Access Procedure

A device does not execute RACH arbitrarily. It is triggered by specific operational events in both idle and active states:

![[RACH_Slide_2.png]]

1. **RRC_IDLE to RRC_CONNECTED Transition:** The UE is camped and needs to establish an active connection (e.g., to make a call, send a text, or start a web session).
2. **Uplink Data Arrival:** The UE is in `RRC_CONNECTED` but has lost its uplink timing synchronization with the base station, and now needs to send new data.
3. **Uplink Sync Lost (Sync Lost):** The gNodeB has downlink data to transmit to the UE, but the UE's uplink synchronization has expired, necessitating a RACH to realign timing.
4. **Synchronization during Handover (Handover):** When a UE transitions from a serving cell to a target cell, it must run a RACH in the target cell to achieve uplink synchronization with the new antenna array.

---

## 🤝 2. Contention-Based Random Access (CBRA)

Contention-Based Random Access (CBRA) is the standard method used when multiple devices share the same pool of preambles. Because UEs select preambles randomly, there is a risk of two UEs transmitting the same preamble on the same resource at the same time, leading to a collision (contention).

![[RACH_Slide_3.png]]

CBRA is a highly structured **4-Step Procedure** designed to detect and resolve these collisions:

![[RACH_Slide_4.png]]

---

### Step 1: Random Access Preamble (Msg1 on PRACH)

The UE initiates the procedure by selecting a single preamble signature from a common pool configured in SIB1 and transmitting it on the **Physical Random Access Channel (PRACH)**.

![[RACH_Slide_5.png]]

#### Mathematical & Physical Mechanisms:
* **Timing Adjustment (TA) Estimation:** Because UEs are at different distances from the gNodeB, their signals arrive with different propagation delays. The UE estimates its starting propagation delay based on the received SSB timing.
* **Power Control & Ramping:** The UE measures the path loss on the downlink SSB (Open-Loop Power Control) to estimate the starting transmission power. If the gNodeB does not respond to Msg1, the UE assumes the power was too low, backs off, increases its power by a set step size (**Power Ramping**), and transmits again.
* **Guard Time:** The preamble sequence contains a guard time to ensure that even if the device is far away, its delayed signal will not overlap and interfere with subsequent uplink subframes.

---

### Step 2: Random Access Response (Msg2 on PDSCH)

Upon detecting the preamble, the gNodeB responds by transmitting the **Random Access Response (RAR)** on the PDSCH. The downlink control signal is scrambled and addressed using the **RA-RNTI** (Random Access Radio Network Temporary Identifier), which is tied directly to the exact time-frequency resource the UE used to send Msg1.

![[RACH_Slide_6.png]]

#### The RAR Msg2 Parameters:
* **RAPID (Random Access Preamble Identifier):** Echoes back the specific preamble index the gNodeB detected, allowing the UE to confirm the RAR is meant for its preamble.
* **Timing Advance (TA) Correction:** An explicit, fine-tuned timing correction value calculated by the gNodeB. The UE applies this correction to shift its clock, ensuring its future uplink transmissions arrive exactly aligned with the base station's slot boundaries.
* **Uplink Scheduling Grant (RARG):** Allocates uplink resources on the PUSCH for the UE to transmit Msg3.
* **TC-RNTI (Temporary Cell RNTI):** A temporary identity assigned by the gNodeB.

---

### Step 3: Scheduled Transmission (Msg3 on PUSCH)

Using the exact resource block allocations and timing advance provided in the RAR grant, the UE transmits **Msg3** (Scheduled Transmission) on the PUSCH.

![[RACH_Slide_7.png]]

#### Msg3 Functions and Collision Scenarios:
Msg3 acts as the **RRC Connection Setup Request** (or RRC Resume Request) and carries the UE's unique identity (such as the S-TMSI, a 5G-GUTI slice, or a random value).

* **Scenario A (No Collision):** Two UEs chose *different* preambles in Step 1. The gNodeB detects both, generates separate RAR grants, and each UE transmits Msg3 on isolated PUSCH resources without interference.
* **Scenario B (Contention/Collision):** Two UEs chose the *same* preamble signature in Step 1. Both will decode the *same* RAR Msg2 and transmit their Msg3 on the *same* PUSCH resources using the *same* TC-RNTI.
  * The gNodeB's receiver will see overlapping signals. Due to spatial positioning or signal strength, the gNodeB will typically decode only *one* of the Msg3 packets successfully, while the other is discarded as noise.

---

### Step 4: Contention Resolution (Msg4 on PDSCH)

The gNodeB responds to Msg3 by sending **Msg4** (Contention Resolution), addressed using the TC-RNTI on the PDSCH.

![[RACH_Slide_8.png]]

#### Msg4 Resolution Logic:
Msg4 carries the **Contention Resolution Identity**, which contains a bit-for-bit copy of the unique ID received in the successful Msg3.

* **RA Success:** The UE that successfully sent its Msg3 reads Msg4, sees that the unique ID matches its own, and completes the connection. Its **TC-RNTI is promoted to a permanent C-RNTI** (Cell RNTI), and it enters `RRC_CONNECTED` state.
* **RA Failure:** The losing UE reads Msg4, sees that the ID does not match, realizes a collision occurred, discards the TC-RNTI, backs off for a randomized interval, and restarts the RACH process from Step 1.

---

## 🔒 3. Contention-Free Random Access (CFRA)

To eliminate latency and the risk of collisions during critical operations, 5G NR provides **Contention-Free Random Access (CFRA)**.

![[RACH_Slide_9.png]]

### CFRA Triggers:
CFRA is triggered strictly under network control when the UE's identity is already known:

![[RACH_Slide_10.png]]

1. **Handover:** The network wants to move the UE to a target cell instantly, without the risk of RACH failures.
2. **Downlink Data Arrival:** Downlink data arrives for an active UE that has temporarily lost uplink synchronization.
3. **Non-Standalone (NSA) Mode Addition:** Adding a secondary 5G NR carrier link to an active 4G LTE anchor.

---

### CFRA 3-Step Procedure:

CFRA bypasses the scheduled transmission and contention resolution stages entirely, completing in just **3 steps**:

![[RACH_Slide_11.png]]

* **Step 0: RA Preamble Assignment:** The gNodeB sends an RRC message (e.g., Handover Command) directly to the UE, assigning it a **dedicated preamble signature** reserved exclusively for that specific UE.
* **Step 1: Dedicated RA Preamble (Msg1 on PRACH):** The UE transmits this dedicated preamble on the PRACH during the assigned time window.
* **Step 2: RA Response (Msg2 on PDSCH):** The gNodeB detects the dedicated preamble, calculates the Timing Advance (TA) correction, and sends the RAR on the PDSCH with an uplink grant.
  * Because the preamble was reserved exclusively for this UE, **there is zero risk of collisions or contention.** The UE immediately applies the TA correction, utilizes the C-RNTI, and resumes high-speed data transmission.

---

## 📊 Summary of Random Access Procedures

The 5G NR Random Access architecture optimizes access speed and reliability across different use cases:

![[RACH_Slide_12.png]]

* **Triggers:** Manages connections for initial access (Idle to Connected), uplink sync recovery, and active handovers.
* **CBRA vs. CFRA:**
  * **CBRA (4-Step):** Msg1 (Preamble PRACH) ➔ Msg2 (RAR with TA & TC-RNTI) ➔ Msg3 (Connection Request on PUSCH) ➔ Msg4 (Contention Resolution). Uses random preambles and resolves collisions via unique IDs.
  * **CFRA (3-Step):** Step 0 (Dedicated preamble assignment) ➔ Msg1 (Dedicated Preamble PRACH) ➔ Msg2 (RAR with TA correction & UL grant). Bypasses Msg3/Msg4, ensuring 100% collision-free access.

![[RACH_Slide_13.png]]
