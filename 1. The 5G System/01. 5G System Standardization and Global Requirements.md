# 01. 5G System Standardization and Global Requirements

Standardization is a massive international effort aimed at "connecting all the world’s people." This comprehensive guide covers the global cellular standardization landscape, the technical requirements of 5G, the organizational structures driving it, and the roadmap toward future systems.

![[Materials/Slides/Slide_1.png]]

---

## 🔌 The Power Outlet Problem (Why We Need Standards)

A little over a century ago, as electric grids were built around the world, different countries designed their own power plugs and outlets. Because there was no unified global effort, we ended up with a massive headache: Type A, Type B, Type C, Type D, Type E, Type F, Type G, Type H, Type I, Type J, Type K, and Type L plugs. When you travel today, your devices won't work without an adapter.

If every country or region developed its own unique telecommunications technology, we would have the exact same disaster:
* **No global roaming:** Your phone wouldn’t work when you stepped off a plane in another country.
* **Insane costs:** Companies would constantly duplicate engineering efforts, making technology incredibly expensive.
* **Subpar tech:** Fragmented R&D means global innovation isn't pulled together to find the absolute best solutions.

To avoid this in telecommunications, global organizations step in to establish a single, unified roadmap.

## 🇺🇳 The Visionary: ITU (International Telecommunication Union)

The process starts at the **United Nations** level with the **ITU**. Established in 1865 as the International Telegraph Union, it has evolved to manage modern ICTs (Information and Communication Technologies).

### ITU Structure
The ITU is divided into three key sectors:
1. **ITU-T (Standardization):** Telecommunication Standards. Handles things like cybersecurity, machine learning, and video compression standards.
2. **ITU-R (Radiocommunications):** **The critical player for 5G**. Focuses on Radiocommunication and manages how international radio frequency spectrums and satellite orbits are allocated.
3. **ITU-D (Development):** Telecommunication Development. Focuses on creating policies, regulations, and training programs for developing countries.

## 📊 Defining the 5G "Dream" (IMT-2020)

The ITU doesn't actually design the technology or write the engineering blueprints; they define the **performance requirements**. In 2015, the ITU convened a forum called **IMT-2020** to outline what the "5G generation" must achieve. They mapped these targets out on a **spider chart** comparing 4G (**IMT-Advanced**) to the new 5G (**IMT-2020**) expectations:

| Performance Metric | 4G (IMT-Advanced) | 5G (IMT-2020) | Improvement Factor / Focus |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Peak Data Rate** | 1 Gbps | **20 Gbps** | 20x improvement |
| **User Experienced Data Rate** | 10 Mbps | **100 Mbps** | 10x improvement (realistic average speed) |
| **Latency** | 10 ms | **1 ms** | 10x reduction (end-to-end packet delay) |
| **Mobility** | 350 km/h | **500 km/h** | High-speed train support |
| **Connection Density** | $10^5$ devices/$km^2$ | **$10^6$ (1 million) devices/$km^2$** | Massive Internet of Things (mMTC) |
| **Spectrum Efficiency** | $1x$ baseline | **$3x$ baseline** | 3x more bits transmitted per Hz |
| **Area Traffic Capacity** | 0.1 $Mbps/m^2$ | **10 $Mbps/m^2$** | 100x capacity improvement |
| **Network Energy Efficiency** | $1x$ baseline | **$100x$ improvement** | Extreme energy reduction per transmitted bit |

## 🛠️ The Architects: 3GPP (3rd Generation Partnership Project)

Once the ITU published the IMT-2020 requirements, the industry needed an engineering body to actually build the solution. Enter **3GPP** (www.3gpp.org).

### 3GPP vs 3GPP2
Historically, there were two competing groups:
* **3GPP:** Developed **GSM** (2G), **UMTS** (3G), and **LTE** (4G). It is now the undisputed dominant force behind 5G.
* **3GPP2:** Worked on **cdmaOne** and **CDMA2000** solutions. 

### Organizational Partners
3GPP is a massive cooperative powerhouse where regional standardization bodies (Organizational Partners) and private tech giants (vendors, operators, chipset makers) co-create blueprints:
* **ETSI** (Europe): European Telecommunications Standards Institute
* **ATIS** (USA): Alliance for Telecommunications Industry Solutions
* **CCSA** (China): China Communications Standards Association
* **TSDSI** (India): Telecommunications Standards Development Society India
* **ARIB** & **TTC** (Japan): Association of Radio Industries and Businesses & Telecommunication Technology Committee
* **TTA** (South Korea): Telecommunications Technology Association

*Note: 3GPP2 was historically backed by ARIB, CCSA, TTA, TTC, and the North American **TIA** (Telecommunications Industry Association).*

## 🧬 3GPP Internal Structure & Working Groups

To coordinate this massive design task, 3GPP divides the work under the **Project Coordination Group (PCG)** into three **Technical Specification Groups (TSGs)**, which are further divided into specialized **Working Groups (WGs)**:

### 1. TSG RAN (Radio Access Network)
Focuses strictly on wireless communication, physical layers, and the over-the-air physics.
* **RAN WG1:** Radio Layer 1 specifications (Physical layer, coding, waveforms, channel structures).
* **RAN WG2:** Radio Layer 2 (MAC/RLC/PDCP) and Radio Layer 3 RRC (Radio Resource Control) specifications.
* **RAN WG3:** Network Interfaces (Iub spec, Iur spec, Iu spec), UTRAN O&M requirements, and Radio/Core Network interfaces.
* **RAN WG4:** Radio Performance and Protocol aspects (RF parameters, BS conformance, coexistence).
* **RAN WG5:** Mobile Terminal Conformance Testing (UE testing specs).
* **RAN WG6:** GSM EDGE Radio Access Network (legacy maintenance).

### 2. TSG CT (Core Network & Terminals)
Handles backend heavy-lifting like user mobility protocols, smart cards, and core network interfaces.
* **CT WG1:** Mobility Management, Call Control, and Session Management (MM/CC/SM) (Iu, end-to-end aspects).
* **CT WG3:** Interworking with external networks.
* **CT WG4:** Protocols within the Core Network (MAP, GTP, BCH, SS).
* **CT WG6:** Smart Card Application Aspects (SIM/USIM card technologies).

### 3. TSG SA (Service & System Aspects)
Coordinates overall architecture, multimedia codecs, and system-wide management.
* **SA WG1:** Services (defining user requirements and service concepts).
* **SA WG2:** Architecture (overall system topology, SBA, network functions design).
* **SA WG3:** Security (security, privacy, and encryption protocols).
* **SA WG4:** Speech, audio, video, and multimedia Codecs.
* **SA WG5:** Telecom Management (network operations and orchestration).
* **SA WG6:** Mission-Critical Applications (public safety, MCX services).

## ⏳ Standardization Sprints: The 5G Timeline

3GPP operates in **12-to-18-month "sprints"** called **Releases**. Each Release rolls out a stable, fully fleshed-out package of features. 

* **2016 - 2017:** Evaluations and requirements study phase (Deliverable: **3GPP Release 13 & Release 14**).
* **2018 - 2019:** Technical specification, system work, and ongoing evolution (Deliverable: **3GPP Release 15 & Release 16**).
* **2020:** Official submission and acceptance of the design as the global **ITU IMT-2020** 5G standard.

*Source Reference:* Revised from the ITU-T workshop documentation by Erik Guttman ([ITU-T Workshop Document](https://www.itu.int/en/lTU-T/Workshops-and-Seminars/201807/Documents/3_Erik_Guttman.pdf)).

## 📈 The Cellular Generations & Releases Timeline

Every major cellular generation takes roughly 10 years to completely manifest, often hitting an incremental "halfway" generation mark along the way:

```
1999 (3G) ──────> 2002 (3.5G) ──────> 2011 (Real 4G) ──────> 2016 (4.5G) ──────> 2019 (5G) ──────> 2021 (5.5G) ──────> 2027+ (6G)
```

* **3G (Universal Mobile Telecommunications System - UMTS):** Started in **1999** with **3GPP Release 99**, eventually evolving to **3.5G** (HSDPA/HSUPA/HSPA) in **2002** with **Release 5**.
* **4G (Long Term Evolution - LTE):** Arrived in **2009** with **Release 8 (LTE)**. The transition to "Real 4G" (**LTE-Advanced**) occurred in **2011** with **Release 10**, and evolved to **4.5G (LTE-Advanced Pro)** in **2016** with **Release 13**.
* **5G (New Radio - NR):** Officially kicked off with **Release 15 in 2019** (Phase 1 5G), and fully matured into Phase 2 5G in **2020** with **Release 16**.
* **5.5G (5G-Advanced):** Iterative evolution starting in **2021** with **Release 17** and **Release 18**, paving the way for ultra-low latency and advanced industrial IoT enhancements.
* **6G Roadmap:** Official studies and requirements definition will begin in **2027** around **Release 21** and beyond, setting the baseline for the next turn-of-the-decade wireless generation.

*(Note: On the timeline slide, red text/entries denote future dates and features that are not yet officially confirmed).*
