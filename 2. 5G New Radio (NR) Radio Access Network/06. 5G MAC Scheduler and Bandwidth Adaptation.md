# 06. 5G MAC Scheduler and Bandwidth Adaptation

The **MAC Scheduler** is the centralized brain within the gNodeB base station responsible for distributing radio access across the time and frequency domains. 5G NR operates on an **OFDMA (Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access)** downlink architecture, allowing multiple users to concurrently share the system bandwidth by being assigned specific time-frequency resource grids.

![[Materials/Slides/Scheduler_Slide_1.png]]

---

## 🗺️ What is the MAC Scheduler?

The physical channel over the air is represented as a complex time-frequency resource grid consisting of subcarriers (frequency domain) and slots (time domain). 

![[Materials/Slides/Scheduler_Slide_2.png]]

The MAC scheduler dynamically partitions this resource grid, allocating specific resource block blocks to active devices (User Equipment / UEs) to coordinate multi-user shared channel access.

### Inputs and Outputs of the Scheduling Engine:

The centralized scheduling engine processes continuous feedback loops to output real-time allocation matrices:

![[Materials/Slides/Scheduler_Slide_3.png]]

* **Scheduler Inputs:** 
  * **Channel Conditions:** Downlink measurements reported by the UE, and uplink sounding signals measured by the gNodeB.
  * **Buffer Status (Uplink Only):** Queue metrics sent by the UE indicating pending data.
  * **Antenna Resource Assignment:** Spatial beamforming and Massive MIMO array constraints.
* **Scheduler Outputs (Allocations):**
  * **Scheduled UEs:** Active devices authorized to transmit or receive in the slot.
  * **Resource Blocks (RBs):** Precise time-frequency subcarrier segments granted.
  * **Transport Format:** Transport Block Size (TBS), modulation scheme (MCS), coding rate, rate matching parameters, and antenna port mapping.

## 🏎️ 1. Receiver-Bandwidth Adaptation: Bandwidth Parts (BWP)

One of the most powerful efficiency features managed by the 5G MAC layer is **Bandwidth Adaptation using Bandwidth Parts (BWP)**.

![[Materials/Slides/Scheduler_Slide_7.png]]

### The Engineering Problem:
In 5G NR, a single radio carrier can have an exceptionally wide operational RF bandwidth (e.g., 100 MHz in Sub-6 GHz or up to 400 MHz in mmWave). If a smartphone (User Equipment / UE) is forced to constantly monitor the full 100 MHz channel just to check for background emails or text messages, its radio frontend components will experience massive power consumption, draining the device battery rapidly.

### The BWP Solution:
5G introduces the concept of a **Bandwidth Part (BWP)**—a contiguous subset of subcarriers defined within the overall wideband carrier spectrum.

* **Dynamic Switching:** A device can be configured with up to 4 BWPs per carrier (e.g., `BWP#1` and `BWP#2`), but only **one single BWP can be active at any given moment**.
* **Low-Power Monitoring (`BWP#1` Active):** When a user is not actively downloading large files, the gNodeB commands the phone to switch to a narrow, highly optimized bandwidth part (`BWP#1`). The phone only powers its RF circuitry to listen to this thin slice of spectrum, drastically reducing energy usage.
* **High-Throughput Burst (`BWP#2` Active):** The exact microsecond a massive data burst arrives (like opening a 4K video stream), the MAC scheduler triggers a **Switch of Active BWP**, prompting the phone to open its receiver wide to a high-capacity bandwidth part (`BWP#2`). Once the download completes, the network swiftly switches the device back to the narrow `BWP#1` to resume power saving.

## 🛑 2. Downlink Scheduling: Preemption (URLLC Interception)

5G is designed to handle mixed traffic requirements simultaneously. To support **URLLC (Ultra-Reliable Low-Latency Communications)** alongside traditional high-speed data (eMBB), the 5G MAC scheduler implements a mechanism called **Preemption**.

![[Materials/Slides/Scheduler_Slide_4.png]]

### The Injection Scenario:
Imagine `Device A` is a standard smartphone actively receiving a large, high-throughput mobile broadband download (eMBB) spanning an entire radio slot.
Suddenly, a mission-critical emergency or a factory command arises for `Device B` (a URLLC node). Because URLLC traffic has an unyielding **1-millisecond latency requirement**, the network cannot afford to wait for `Device A`'s radio slot to fully conclude.

![[Materials/Slides/Scheduler_Slide_8.png]]

### The Preemption Mechanism:
1. **The Hijack:** The gNodeB's MAC scheduler instantly punches a hole straight through the middle of `Device A`'s ongoing slot transmission to forcefully inject `Device B`'s critical data packet (`Device B preempting A`).
2. **The Damage to Device A:** Because its allocated frequency resource was hijacked mid-transmission, `Device A` receives corrupted data symbols in those specific resource blocks.
3. **The Preemption Indicator:** To prevent `Device A` from wasting valuable processing cycles attempting to decode a fundamentally corrupted transport block, the gNodeB transmits a specific control element called a **Preemption Indicator** on the physical downlink control channel.
4. **The Soft Buffer Flush:** The Preemption Indicator uses a bit-map sequence (e.g., `00100000110000`) to point directly to the exact time-frequency resource structures that were compromised. Upon reading this indicator, **`Device A` instantly flushes that specific part of its soft buffer memory**. This tells its internal tracking engine to disregard the corrupted segments during the next Hybrid-ARQ combining cycle, paving the way for a clean retransmission of just the punctured blocks.

## 🚦 3. Downlink Scheduling Paradigms: Dynamic vs. Configured (SPS)

To satisfy different application traffic shapes, the 5G MAC scheduler offers two completely distinct allocation philosophies: **Dynamic Scheduling** and **Configured Scheduling (Semi-Persistent Scheduling / SPS)**.

![[Materials/Slides/Scheduler_Slide_9.png]]

### Philosophy A: Dynamic Scheduling (Without Configured Scheduling)
This is the standard mode of operation optimized for highly variable, unpredictable internet traffic.
* **The Call Flow:** For every single radio slot, the User Equipment (UE) must actively **Monitor the PDCCH** (Physical Downlink Control Channel).
* **Per-Slot Authorization:** The gNodeB must continuously transmit a **DCI (Downlink Control Information)** message masked with the device’s unique identifier, the **C-RNTI** (Cell Radio Network Temporary Identifier). This DCI explicitly tells the phone: *"In this exact slot, you have an allocation on the PDSCH (Physical Downlink Shared Channel) with this specific modulation format."*
* **The Drawback:** While highly flexible, running this loop for every individual transmission block generates a massive amount of ongoing control plane signaling overhead.

### Philosophy B: Configured Scheduling / Semi-Persistent Scheduling (SPS)
SPS is designed for predictable, recurring traffic profiles that look identical from packet to packet, such as fixed-interval **Voice over 5G (VoNR)** streams or periodic industrial sensor updates.
* **The Setup (RRC Signaling):** The network uses a permanent Layer 3 **RRC Signaling** command to establish an overall template for the connection, setting a fixed **periodicity** (e.g., *"Expect an allocation every 20 milliseconds"*).
* **Activating the Configuration:** The UE monitors the PDCCH. The gNodeB shoots a specialized activation **DCI message**, this time masked with a custom **CS-RNTI (Configured Scheduling RNTI)**. This locks the configuration in place, initializing the baseline channel configurations.
* **No PDCCH Phase (The Payload Pipe):** Once activated, the gNodeB transmits data over the PDSCH at the exact agreed-upon time intervals **without sending any accompanying PDCCH or DCI control messages**. The phone knows exactly when to tune in, liberating massive amounts of control channel spectrum for other users.
* **The Overwrite Mechanism:** If a sudden burst of high-speed data needs to override the quiet routine scheduling, the gNodeB can simply issue a standard dynamic DCI masked with the regular **C-RNTI**. If received by the phone, this **overwrites the active Configured Scheduling constraints** for that slot, seamlessly expanding data bandwidth on demand.

## 📊 4. Downlink Runtime Feedback Loop

To make real-time scheduling decisions, the gNodeB relies on continuous channel measurements and buffering updates:

![[Materials/Slides/Scheduler_Slide_5.png]]

1. **Reference Signal Transmission:** The gNodeB beams down reference signals known as **CSI-RS** (Channel State Information Reference Signal) and **SSB** (Synchronization Signal Block).
2. **UE Measurements:** Devices in the area perform precise measurements on these waveforms:
   * **RSRP (Reference Signal Received Power):** Measures signal strength.
   * **CSI (Channel State Information):** Includes **CQI** (Channel Quality Indicator, indicating supported modulation), **PMI** (Precoding Matrix Indicator, for antenna phase adjustment), and **RI** (Rank Indicator, for MIMO spatial layers).
3. **CSI Reporting:** The UE reports these parameters back to the tower. CSI reports can be configured as *Periodic* (fixed intervals), *Semi-persistent* (active intervals), or *Aperiodic* (triggered on-demand by the gNodeB).
4. **Channel-Dependent Scheduling:** The MAC scheduler parses these channel conditions, executing **Multi-user diversity** scheduling to grant resource blocks to UEs with peak channel metrics, dynamically matching them with the highest possible modulation scheme (e.g., 256QAM) or focusing narrow antenna beams at their locations.

## 📡 5. Uplink Scheduling Mechanics

Uplink scheduling is managed by the gNodeB, but the operational constraints and feedback parameters differ from the downlink:

![[Materials/Slides/Scheduler_Slide_11.png]]

![[Materials/Slides/Scheduler_Slide_12.png]]

### A. Uplink Operational Constraints
* **SRS Sounding:** The base station measures the uplink channel quality using a **Sounding Reference Signal (SRS)** waveform transmitted by the UE (restricted to a maximum of 4 antenna ports, compared to the downlink's 32-port CSI-RS capabilities).
* **Power Limited:** Unlike the base station (which is bandwidth-limited by spectrum rules), mobile devices are **Power Limited** due to battery constraints and RF amplification hardware limits.
* **Per Device Scheduling:** Uplink resource allocation is granted **per device (UE)**, whereas downlink allocation is assigned **per radio bearer (DRB)**. The UE's internal MAC layer determines how to distribute its granted uplink block among its active bearers based on logical channel priorities.

### B. Uplink Scheduling Paradigms (Dynamic vs. Configured Grants)
Similar to downlink scheduling, the uplink supports dynamic allocations and configured grants:

![[Materials/Slides/Scheduler_Slide_13.png]]

#### 1. Dynamic Uplink Scheduling
* **Uplink Grants:** The UE monitors the PDCCH. The gNodeB issues an Uplink Grant (DCI masked with the device's C-RNTI).
* **Transmission:** The UE multiplexes its RLC buffers and transmits on the **PUSCH (Physical Uplink Shared Channel)**.

#### 2. Configured Grants (Uplink SPS equivalent)
To support regular VoNR or periodic sensor traffic, 5G NR defines two configured grant types:
* **Configured Grant Type 1:** 
  * The entire uplink resource allocation, periodicity, MCS, and CS-RNTI parameters are configured **completely via RRC signaling**.
  * No dynamic activation DCI is required. The phone simply transmits on the PUSCH at the configured periodic intervals automatically.
* **Configured Grant Type 2:**
  * The periodicity is configured via RRC, but the PUSCH resources must be initialized and locked in by a **dynamic activation DCI** message masked with the **CS-RNTI** (matching downlink SPS behavior).
* **Dynamic Overwrite:** In both types, a standard dynamic DCI masked with the regular **C-RNTI** can be sent to **overwrite the active Configured Grant** for that slot, providing instant scheduling flexibility.
