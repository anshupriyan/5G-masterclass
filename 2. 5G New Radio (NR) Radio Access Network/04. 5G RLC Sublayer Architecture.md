# 04. 5G RLC Sublayer Architecture

The **Radio Link Control (RLC)** layer is a critical component of the 5G NR Layer 2 protocol stack. It receives **PDCP PDUs** from the layer above, which it treats as **RLC SDUs**. After processing, it outputs **RLC PDUs** down to the **MAC (Medium Access Control)** layer.

![[RLC_Slide_1.png]]

---

## 🗺️ The Two Fundamental Mandates of RLC

According to the 3GPP specifications, the RLC layer is engineered to handle two primary responsibilities:

![[RLC_Slide_2.png]]

1. **Segmentation and Reassembly**
2. **ARQ (Automatic Repeat Request) Retransmissions**

### The Key Architectural Shift in 5G: No In-Sequence Delivery at RLC
A major difference between 4G LTE and 5G NR is that **5G RLC does not handle in-sequence delivery or packet reordering.**

![[RLC_Slide_5.png]]

![[RLC_Slide_6.png]]

* **Why?** Forcing the RLC layer to reorder packets adds significant processing latency and drastically increases the hardware buffering/memory requirements at the base station.
* **The 5G Solution:** To achieve ultra-low latency, the reordering and in-sequence delivery functions were completely stripped out of the RLC layer and moved up to the **PDCP** layer. 5G RLC focuses purely on fast segmentation and error correction.

---

## ⚙️ The Three Operational Modes of RLC

Different network applications have completely different data integrity demands. To support them all, RLC can be configured by the network into three distinct operational modes:

![[RLC_Slide_3.png]]

### 1. TM (Transparent Mode)
In this mode, the RLC layer is completely passive and behaves as a transparent pipe.
* **Mechanisms:** It performs **no retransmissions**, **no duplicate detection**, and **no segmentation or reassembly**. The data packet passes through the layer completely untouched.
* **Primary Use Case:** It is strictly used for Control Plane broadcast and common signaling channels, specifically:
  * **BCCH** (Broadcast Control Channel)
  * **CCCH** (Common Control Channel)
  * **PCCH** (Paging Control Channel)
* **Why?** These system channels are pre-designed with robust channel coding to handle varying radio conditions directly at the physical layer, making RLC segmentation and retransmissions unnecessary.

### 2. UM (Unacknowledged Mode)
UM provides structural adaptation without waiting for feedback.
* **Mechanisms:** It supports **segmentation and reassembly**, but **does not perform any retransmissions**. If a packet fragment is lost over the air, the UM entity simply discards it and moves on.
* **Primary Use Case:** Used for real-time, delay-sensitive applications where a small amount of packet loss is acceptable, but retransmission delays would ruin the user experience. The primary example is **VoIP / VoNR (Voice over New Radio)** and streaming services.

### 3. AM (Acknowledged Mode)
AM is the most robust, heavy-duty mode of operation, ensuring absolute data integrity.
* **Mechanisms:** It supports the full suite of RLC capabilities: **segmentation, reassembly, duplicate removal, and ARQ retransmissions** based on receiver feedback.
* **Primary Use Case:** This is the main mode of operation for the **Downlink Shared Channel (DL-SCH)**, used for error-free file transfers, web browsing, app data, and any traffic where missing even a single bit corrupts the entire payload.

## ✂️ The Mechanics of Segmentation

The physical airwaves are constantly fluctuating. The MAC layer below dynamically calculates a strict "radio resource budget" for the next millisecond, telling RLC exactly how many bytes can fit into the upcoming **Transport Block**.

![[RLC_Slide_4.png]]

* **The Process:** If an incoming upper-layer packet (RLC SDU) is larger than the byte allocation granted by the MAC scheduler, the RLC layer slices the packet into smaller fragments called **segments**.
* **The Header Mapping:** To ensure the receiving side can stitch these fragments back together, RLC maps tracking parameters into the RLC PDU header:
  * **Sequence Number (SN):** Identifies the overall packet sequence.
  * **SI (Segmentation Information):** A field indicating whether the PDU contains a complete SDU, the first segment, a middle segment, or the last segment of an SDU.
  * **SO (Segmentation Offset):** A precise byte-offset pointer that tells the receiver exactly where this specific fragment fits within the original, unsegmented RLC SDU payload.

## 🔁 The ARQ Retransmission Protocol (Step-by-Step Timeline)

When running in **Acknowledged Mode (AM)**, RLC uses a sliding window and a feedback loop to repair data errors that the lower physical/MAC layers fail to correct.

Let's trace the exact timeline ($T_0$ through $T_6$) of an over-the-air packet drop and recovery:

```
[Transmitter Window]                                        [Receiver Window]
  Starts at PDU 'n'                                           Waiting for 'n'
         ↓                                                           ↓
      ( T0: Transmission of block 'n' through 'n+2' over the air )
         ↓                                                           ↓
  [n] [n+1] [n+2] ------------(Wireless Drop on n+1)------------>  [n] [  ] [n+2] 
                                                            (T1: Error detected!)
                                                            (T2: Missing hole tracked)
         ↓                                                           ↓
      ( T3: Transmitter keeps sending 'n+3' to 'n+5'; sets poll flag on 'n+5' )
         ↓                                                           ↓
  [n+3] [n+4] [n+5] -------------------------------------------->  [n+3] [n+4] [n+5]
                                                            (T4: Receiver hits flag)
                                                                     │
                                                                     ▼
                                                          Compiles Status Report:
                                                          "ACK up to n, NACK n+1"
                                                                     │
         ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
         ▼
(T5: Transmitter processes Status Report)
  - Advances window to start at 'n+3'
  - Retransmits missing 'n+1'
         │
         ▼
(T6: Retransmissions successfully land)
  - Receiver window fully cleared!
```

---

### Granular Phase Breakdown:

#### 🕒 Time $T_0$ (Initial State)
The transmitter has a queue of PDUs waiting for initial transmission. The sliding **Transmit Window** is positioned, starting at PDU $n$. The transmitter packs and flashes PDUs $n$, $n+1$, and $n+2$ over the air.

![[RLC_Slide_7.png]]

* *Transmit Window State:* Tracks sent but not yet acknowledged packets ($n$, $n+1$, $n+2$) alongside the pending transmission queue.

---

#### 🕒 Time $T_1$ (The Radio Intercept)
At the receiving end, PDU $n$ and PDU $n+2$ arrive successfully. However, due to sudden localized radio interference, **PDU $n+1$ is completely lost or corrupted**.

![[RLC_Slide_8.png]]

* *MAC Interconnect:* Either the lower-layer hardware HARQ loop is handling the repair, or the physical layer failed to detect the bit-level error entirely, passing the corrupted frame up.

---

#### 🕒 Time $T_2$ (Tracking the Hole)
The receiver processes $n$ and $n+2$. Because $n+1$ is missing, a "hole" is identified in the receiver's state tracking matrix.

![[RLC_Slide_9.png]]

* *Receiver State:* The receiver holds the subsequent block ($n+2$) in its buffer, waiting for the missing piece.

---

#### 🕒 Time $T_3$ (Continuous Flow & Polling)
While waiting for feedback, the transmitter continues pouring data down the pipe, sending out PDUs $n+3$, $n+4$, and $n+5$.

![[RLC_Slide_10.png]]

* **The Poll Trigger:** Because $n+5$ represents the absolute last piece of data currently sitting in the transmitter’s transmission buffer, the transmitter needs to confirm everything arrived safely. It sets a specific **Poll Flag** in the RLC header of PDU $n+5$.

---

#### 🕒 Time $T_4$ (The Status Report Trigger)
The receiver successfully ingests $n+3$, $n+4$, and $n+5$. It reads the header of $n+5$ and notices the Poll Flag is checked. This forces the receiver to instantly compile an official **RLC Status Report**.

![[RLC_Slide_11.png]]

* *Control Signaling Output:* The report contains an explicit map: It issues an Acknowledgement (ACK) confirming everything up to PDU $n$ is clear, but attaches a Negative Acknowledgement (NACK) explicitly calling out that **PDU $n+1$ is missing**. This control message is prioritized and shot back to the transmitter.

---

#### 🕒 Time $T_5$ (Window Advancement & Correction)
The transmitter receives and parses the RLC Status Report.

![[RLC_Slide_12.png]]

* **Window Advancement:** Because the report guarantees that everything up to PDU $n$ arrived safely, the transmitter securely clears those blocks from its memory and **advances its Transmit Window to start at $n+3$**.
* **Retransmission:** Simultaneously, seeing the NACK for $n+1$, the transmitter fetches $n+1$ from its backup buffer and **retransmits it over the air**.

---

#### 🕒 Time $T_6$ (Final Resolution)
The retransmitted PDU $n+1$ lands cleanly at the receiver. The receiver’s reassembly engine plugs the hole.

![[RLC_Slide_13.png]]

* *Reassembly Complete:* All data blocks up to $n+5$ are now fully accounted for, marked as successfully received, and cleared for processing by upper layers.

---

## 📊 Summary of RLC Operations

The RLC sublayer encapsulates, segments, and corrects over-the-air transmission errors:

![[RLC_Slide_14.png]]

* **TM (Transparent Mode):** No reassembly, no segmentation, no retransmissions. Strictly for BCCH, CCCH, and PCCH.
* **UM (Unacknowledged Mode):** Supports segmentation and reassembly, but no retransmissions. Used for delay-sensitive, loss-tolerant traffic (VoNR).
* **AM (Acknowledged Mode):** Supports the full suite of segmentation, reassembly, and ARQ feedback retransmissions. Used for reliable data transfer (DL-SCH).
